Lake Superior camping and kayak trip diary.

D204-044 08-01-27 - N40 - Superior diary
JULY 6 Friday. 1230am nearing the Soo. Camp last night at Batman's Tent and RV park. Arrive 10pm-ish. Seems to be nobody home. Flashes of lightning in the distance. Chatty proprietor eventually turns up. Fifth wheel trailer in line ahead of us, also chatty. Adoni discreetly trying to speed up the process.

Rain in the air. Drizzle as we put up the tents. Can't figure out how to get the poles inserted in the dark. I do it the wrong way several times in a row. Haven't felt so stupid and frustrated in years. Miriam and Adonis eventually have to show us how. Merle's headlamp a godsend. Otherwise we'd still be stumbling about in the dark.

Get the tent pitched, bedding arranged. Crawl into the sack. Discover we are on the edge of a ditch and a set of very deep tire ruts. Put on our underwerar again, drag the tent sideways. Finally achieve a decent level of comfort.

Weather calms down. A frog glug, glug, glugging all night. Loons in the distance. Wake several times but comfortable. My usual coffee and porridge on the SVEA stove this morning.

D204-045 08-01-27 -
To Espanola for brekkie. Wonderful place opp the OPP station. Onward to the Trans Canada Highway. Buy another headlamp at the Canadian Tire store in Espanola, although I trust we'll be more efficient at tent-pitching tonight.

JULY 7 Saturday. Arrive at Gargantua Bay 5-6pm last night. Almost a kilometer of sand. An island at the mouth of the bay with a small navigation light. Unload the car at the boat launch parking lot. Assemble one kayak. Ferry the other kayak, still packed in its cases, plus gear down the beach to our selected campsite. We set up in time for a small campfire. Mosquitoes ferocious. Slather in Deet.

Today, up at 7 for the usual dilithium and porridge. Then off paddling to Warp Bay in the kayaks. More lovely campsites. Back against headwinds, a light swell from the Lake Superior verging on whitecaps when we turn into Gargantua Bay.

A lazy aft beachcombing. Oh, saw a wreck in Gargantua Bay this morning. We paddled over and round it. Ribs sticking out of the water and two huge iron cylinders from the engine.

JULY 8 Sunday. Mixed weather, rain in the morning. Breakfast, DGF, reading.

D204-046 08-01-27 -
Afternoon, paddle south in off and on sun. A slight swell coming in from the lake. Refreshing, invigorating, scary? The water has a wonderful blue-green colour. Sandy bottom near our campsite changing to huge boulders southward. Down the coast, left of the bay when looking west, ochre and grey cliffs, fantastically broken in places. Some places, smoothed by wave or glacier. Layers, mixed, twisted. Scenes from a Bartram print.

Get the kayak into one narrow cleft with not enough room to turn. Have to back out. One or two "bathtubs" down here. Perfect for a hot afternoon's skinny. A prime spot for a picnic lunch away from our camp on the beach.

Merle and I back to camp via the parking lot to get stuff out of the car. Miriam and Adonis go round the small island in the bay. Practice my over-the-side exit. Semi-wet-suit pants a partial success. A real "farmer john" wet-suit, tighter fitting, would probably be even better. Ability to jump out into the water gives a feeling of being master of the situation. Otherwise every launch and landing threatens to be ignominious. Especially when beaching the kayak on a problematic shore with rocks or waves.

D204-047 08-01-27 -
Haven't decided on our plans for tomorrow. Tonight is the last night paid for. Miriam has a sore hand from a fall on the rocks while walking out to the northerly point enclosing Gargantua Harbour.

JULY 9 Monday. We decamp Gargantua Harbour Beach en route to Warp Bay. We are now on a shallow sand dune at the head of the bay. A long view south, back toward the mouth of Gargantua Harbour. More shelter here, more interesting islands in the offing.

We have a late lunch after about an hour and a half paddle getting here. Some food and equipment had to go back to the car before starting out. Now we can't easily reteat to the car to replenish supples. We are fully committed.

After lunch, another paddle round toward Cape Gargantua. Didn't quite make the whole distance before turning back, but the route seems inviting. Will try again tomorrow. On the way back to camp, Merle and Derek paddle across to Devil's Warehouse Island, the ocher mine island. Glide along beneath steep cliffs, brilliantly coloured by mineral and lichen deposits, and dropping sheer into the water.

D204-048 08-01-27 -
An elemental quality about certain slabs of rock can elicit an almost religious feeling. Almost? Actually! It's like sudden revelation of some hidden aspect of Being. Normally it would probably be difficult to experience something that was everywhere, that permeated everyday experience, that was masked by a thousand other dusty details. But huge rocks, mountain tops, lakes, river, skies seem to blow away the dust, at least for a while.

The ancients sometimes claimed that the planets moving in their orbits emitted the musical tones of a celestial harmony. But we couldn't hear this because it was always present. The mind filtered it out. (It doesn't matter that sound waves can't be transmitted in a vacuum.)

Am now sitting on the sand in one of those pinch-your-bum, light weight folding camp chairs. Hardly a chair, it's right on the ground, but it does give back support. Hard to get enough of this scene. Easy to keep gazing and gazing. A fishing boat hovers down near the mouth of Warp Bay. Bird songs all around. Every treed island seems to have its own voices. Miriam is gathering firewood for this evening.

I think if we were alone I'd try to talk Merle into taking her clothes off for some sun, sand and skin pictures. Probably have to use the old reliable "artist position" to avoid serious embarrassment later on. You know, the Rokeby Venus posture. Emphasize the tactile qualities without displaying any chancy bits.

D204-049 08-01-27 -
This is worth another trip. Plan a more extended but still leisurely route. Seeing this up close, I'm not sure if the old inland haunts quite compare? Well, yes, OSA Lake, for one location certainly does. But the openness of this scene, the larger scale is impressive. A narrow space between two islands, then open water for a hundred miles or more.

Con trail of a plane heading south. Had similar thoughts on the Pukasaw hike, of being, at last, far away from the rat race.

JULY 10 Tuesday. Threats of weather on the marine radio. Decide to make a dash for Cape Gargantua in the morning. Cloudy-dull. An easy paddle. We can't see what's so special about our destination until the last minute. "The Devils's Chair" has two holes pierced low in the back, reminding me of a two-holer outhouse seat. I'll bet the voyageurs called this "The Devil's Privy" or some such. Paddle back against light headwinds after inspecting the local campsites. These are secluded but claustrophobic after Warp Bay.

Turns out after we get back, that Miriam and Adonis were hallooing at us from the top of the cliff overlooking "The Devil's Chair". An inland hiking trail leads to this point from Warp Bay.

D204-050 08-01-27 -
After lunch all go on a hike to Chalfant Cove. Three Km on the map but seems longer. Find a pathetic memorial cross and a message in a bottle on the beach. I Open this for a look. Then it begins to rain. All the way home and into the early evening with a couple of hours respite around campfire time. This we start the hoser way, with gasoline, and with much huffing and puffing to keep it going.

Waves pound on the beach all night. Wind rattles the tent. By now we're fairly damp around the edges, what with our soggy walk back from Chalfant Cove, and miscellaneous small tent leaks. But the sleeping bags are still warm.

July 11 Wednesday. The weather gradually tapers off by about 9:30am. Breakfast while listening to weather forecasts on the marine radio. Small craft warnings, but a west or NW wind is favourable. Time to abandon the outpost. I stroll dowen the the beach to find our kayak half buried in sand thrown up by the waves overnight. Lucky it didn't get washed away. Miriam and Adonis had the foresight to move theirs to higher ground last night.

We pack and leave by noon. Tail winds, a swell from the lake. Especially big waves when coming into Gargantua Harbour. We have the distinction of doing white water kayaking on Lake Superior.

Am unable to take any pictures on this leg. Don't dare release my grip on the paddle even for a few seconds. At one point we seem to make agonizingly slow progress getting lined up to enter the channel between Gargantua Harbour's island and the north point. The waves begin hitting us diagonally, but the prospect of short-cutting through the wild surf beating against the rocks off the port beam isn't too inviting either. Some of these waves are also reflecting off the rocks and rebounding to create a chaos of criss-crossing wave crests. Eventually we make the turn inland and practically surf shoreward, gaining momentum and sliding down the inclined fronts of the waves.

Meanwhile Miriam and Adoni have disappeared in the confusion, creating a certain anxiety when we have time to think about it. On the crests we get a fine view all around. No sign of them. Down in the troughs you can't see the land at all, only the blue-green flanks of the waves and the sky above.

Emerging into calmer waters behind the shelter of the island, we spy Miriam and Adoni on the beach already unloading. They have paddled on the south side of the island and were invisible to us from the north side. We take a couple of big ones over the side while landing. But my trick of jumping out chest deep to stablilize the kayak seems to work. Just don't get wedged in between a loaded canoe or kayak and the shore while you're being pounded by surf. Until you experience it, you probably have no idea of the titanic weight of a loaded or partially swamped boat as it is pounded by breaking waves. Eventually we get packed up with only minor contusions and on the road again.

Tonight we're at Hattie Cove, Pukaskwa National Park, campsite #32. In to Marathon the do laundry and eat. We have an awful time following directions to get to the laundry located on a back street. To the locals it's in the same building as the piano store, right in plain sight.

D204-051 08-01-27 -
JULY 12 Thursday. Rain. In to Marathon again for lunch at a cute deli. Shopping at Canadian Tire, groceries. Back at camp, a walk around the point in back of our campsite. In the rain, lush with many wild flowers we couldn't identify, interesting rocks. Have neglected to bring wildflower identification books.

Supper. Soup. More walking. Sun back again. Photos on the beach. Looks a good sport for kayak launching. Weather radio promises much good for tomorrow. Hot showers after supper. Merle says, take the handicapped stall, more room. Sips of rye whiskey at dusk. Even the mosquitoes seem loveable tonight.

Desiderata: Derek wants a warm fuzzy, with hood. A "hoodie" like kids and Wizard of Id peasants wear. With long arms so he doesn't have to wear mits in July. And a water bottle like Miriam and Adonis have. Should we use the spray skirt in the kayak? Don't the Ojibwa have some kind of Santa Claus myth that Derek can embrace.

We were last in this park in 1981 on the Nature Canada Pukasaw backpacking trip. At that time the facilities of the park were limited to interior camping and canoeing. Leaving from Heron Bay, we were dropped off with our guides at Fisherman's cove, miles to the south of here, to walk back along the coast. A wonderful trip. Today we have a chance to see some of the same coastline from an offshore point of view.

D204-052 08-01-27 -
JULY 13 Oh-oh! Friday the n-teenth. Up late, blue sky. Kayak ass'y in the campground, portage to Horseshoe Bay. Gorgeous sandy beach with regulation driftwood. We put in before lunch. A brief exposure to the outer lake, then we duck in to Hattie Cove through a narrow inlet. The park has a picturesque interpretive centre facing the water. Hattie Cove is a shallow lake, with ducks, loons, water lilies. Lunch against a log on a sandy beach. Cool but comfy if you hunker down beneath the breeze. 12 deg C.

Onward to Pulpwood Harbour. Archipelago of tiny rocky islands to paddle through and explore. A yacht is anchored in the harbour. No odour of fried eggs and ham. They are probably making out in the main cabin. We attempt to continue south to Playter Hbr. A heavy swell on Superior seems a little too exhilirating, so we turn back remembering our escapade re-entering Gargantua Harbour. A frisky entrance to Horseshoe Bay. But no serious difficulty landing. We portage back to camp.

To Marathon for dinner. We are exhausting the dining opportunities in Marathon. Next step will be to knock at the doors of private houses and solicit dinner invitations, or maybe to congregate at the Can. Tire gourmet food counter for beef jerky and granola bars. This is the tail-gate pemmican of latter day voyageurts. Early to bed. Rain begins again at 9pm. Eli, eli ...

More desiderata: a wind shield for the stove, platypus water bottle. New tent, the zipper on our Eureka has finally jammed for good, forcing us the gain entry with a sharp knife. We also need a pocket communicator CB unit that actually works for outgoing messages. One of ours can't talk to the other one.

D204-053 08-01-27 -
JULY 14 Saturday. Rains and rains and never stops/ Forty days and spoils the crops.

We hike to the White River suspension bridge. 3hrs 10mins. A very damp trail through Lake Superior rain forest. Sunny spells. No rain but we brush against wet leaves. The river seems high. A wild white-water chute seethes beneath the bridge and is visible below our boots through protective wire mesh.

The bridge is a real thrill. Probably worth the three hours hard slogging to get there. The campsite of yesteryear is still several km further upstream. 2hrs 50mins back to our present-day campsite.

Our camp in 1981 was a km or so above the bridge, and round the turn in the river that we can see in the distance. But even in that rocky area we had a sandy beach to tent on.

Tired this evening. Stewy concoction for supper. Kayaking prospects on the marine radio for tomorrow seem mediocre.

D204-054 08-01-27 -
JULY 15 Sun-day (a bit). Risque d'orages apres midi. Love-sick crows fornicating in the tree tops. *

We've been making a lot of clever remarks in the last few days. does anybody remember even one of them? I know I said at least 3 smart things recently.

Portaged the few hundred feet from our campsite to the beach. Got launched from Horseshoe Bay. Miriam fell in, unfazed. Launching and landing, it seems, are the most hazardous parts of kayaking.

Apart from a modest and glassy swell, calm conditions. Paddling north, see a bald eagle on a crag. Nattered at by gulls, we are not offended. It is such a lovely morning, we love them. We love everything. Back across the bay, more gentle swells. Lovelier still. Water smooth. Amost no wind. Halcyon weather. The halcyon was a bird that the ancients thought nested right on the wine-dark sea and never needed to come to land. For a while we feel like this too.

Today we make our farthest northing on Lake Superior. On a par with the intrepid Norwesters. Heron Bay, Marathon and Fort William are somewhere up ahead in the blue distance. Sheltering now to write this in a group of tiny islands opposite Hattie Cove. Still 1.5 hours left before the orages that were predicted on the marine radio. What to do with the idle time?

Finished Mary Wesley: Part of the Furniture, yesterday. A favourite book of the last months. The plot comes full circle in a most satisfying way.

* We later find that the peculiar noises of this morning were made by a young crow as it was fed. But to me this sounded like the throes of corvid lust. Tanya Moldauer accuses Richard Peters, my literary alias, of being guilty as usual of the pitiful fallacy. That's "pathetic", Tanya, not pitiful, I-he says.

D204-055 08-01-27 -
See two bears at the side of the road on the way to Marathon for a dinner of Chinese food. Not invited.

JULY 16 Monday. Decamp by 10am and head south. Check out Horsehoe Bay beach just before leaving. Weather perfect for more paddling. Same guy sitting on the beach doing nothing just like yesterday.

Wawa Goose next. One of the seven wonders. Better than the Great Pyramid of Gizeh. (Say, if I built a pyramid in the front yard, would the neighbours call it The Great Pyramid of Geezer?)

Stop at the Petroglyphs Prov. Park. Run out of camera card memory. Need more memory, less weather. We note in passing that Agawa Bay park has 2-3km sandy beach. Inviting. But close to the road. Must check the web for a map to see if some of the campsites may be deeper within the park and more insulated from road noise.

D204-056 08-01-27 -
The spikey-backked critter in the pictograms is Misshepezhieu, the Great Lynx of Ojibwe legend. Have also heard it described as a dragon or water spirit. Sometimes playful and protextive, sometimes dangerous. Don't we know it.

The petroglyph viewing area is staffed by a park interpreter. She has a kind of fishpole handy to pull people out of the water in case they slip off the rocky shelf. I had to go in bare feet to get any decent footing. An iron railing has been removed in the interests of authenticity. She swears that nobody has fallen in on her watch.

Back to the car again, thoughts lurn to logistical details of the trip. Still haven't solved the problem of how to store small doo-dads in the rucksack. It's really more adapted to larger packages. Still need a compartmentalized sack or shoulder bag, perhaps detachable or insertable to contain the Deet bottle, pocket knife, batteries, memory cards, aspirin, etc.

Over the border at the Soo to the Thunderbird Motel in Ignace Michigan. Two people for US$55. Fish dinner on the waterfront. Lake trout with whitefish liver for a side dish. The town is a kind of touristy amalgam of NOTL and Tobermory. A convenient marina. The waterfront stip is full of motels, eateries, bars, antique galleries, sweet shops, plus a few regular stores.

Looks like we'll be driving steadily through Port Huron, Sarnia and home tomorrow. The land here seems very flat after the north of Superior. Going to be an anti-climactic drive. The old fin-de-siecle feeling is setting in. Whither, whence, wherefore?

D204-057 08-01-27 -
JULY 17 Tuesday. Bed springy. DNR this am, but too brief. Hot in the motel room last night. Try using the air conditioning, but sounds like a Boeing 747 taking off. Open the door briefly to let in Ignace, Michigan atmosphere. Back to sleep eventually. Pasty for brekkie in the car, then south on I-75. A pastoral highway, landscape-y but snooze-inducing.

Across the Mackinac Bridge. Spectacular views into Lake Michigan. This point is accessible from Lake Erie without lockage. A repro of Fort Michilimackinacinac is visible below the bridge on the south-western side. Was this fort held by the British for a time during the war?

4pm. Visit Diana at Starbuck's in Sarnia. A quiet moment in her accounting cycle. She is wearing high heels that make her look hugely long-legged. They all discuss corporate stuff: LanXess and Brock University HR practices or malpractices. I began to drift off northward back to our last idyllic paddle out of Horsehoe Bay at Pukaskwa Park. To the place where that halcyon bird nests upon the waters. (And the deer and the antelope play...)

Off again on hwy 402. We should reach Welland and Farr Rd Farm before 9pm.
