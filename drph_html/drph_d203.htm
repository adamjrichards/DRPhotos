2008-01-04 - Chicago, AIA conference.

Jan 4-6, Chicago, AIA conference. * Attended 4 conference sessions. * Visited the Chicago Art Institute. * Dinner with Helen Brown at Macey's. * Gallery of Modern Art. * Walked down to the harbour.
very interesting session, Taylor, UBC on the subject of a certain mosaic which he argues depicts not the Platonic Academy in session but a certain little-known astronomer of the period.

Worst thing: constant encounters with panhandlers. You have to be very cool and ruthless. They try to be hyper-ingratiating and deferential to shame you into making a contribution. Lost $8 per boot to a shoeshine artist because I turned and smiled as he went into his spiel. Had my boots spritzed with polish before I could say no. Then, when the price was announced, had the choice of objecting and making a scene, or paying up.

Weather sunny on Thursday, very cold. Then cloudy and warming. In the 50s of the gesundheit scale on Sunday, our departure day. A few walks, the Art Institute on Saturday with Helen and the Modern Art gallery, our last outing before departure on Sunday.

2008 Jan 5: A singular experience.
After dinner at Macy's with Helen, we encountered a woman falling down in the street near our hotel. Attempted to help her. She was vague about her destination, repeatedly calling someone by name on a nearly deserted street. Blood on her face, looked like a derelict. After waiting around, then attempting to call hotel security, with no result, we tried to get a taxi. One wouldn't take us or say why. Finally we got her into another cab and proceeded to the address she gave. A classy apartment building and here the concierge recognized her but didn't know anything about the "husband" she said was waiting for her upstairs. At that point we left and walked along Chicago's "golden mile", Michigan Avenue. Visited Bloomingdales on the way back to the hotel.

2008 Jan 7: Back from Chicago
7:30hrs door-to-door. 8:30 outbound. Generally positive impression. A BIG city, Toronto-like in its location and perhaps, in its development. The downtown hi-rise core is generally more interesting and walkable than TO. Wide streets, wide sidewalks, buildings seems to have better setback and spacing. It's possible to actually see the tops of skyscrapers, especially along the river.

Despite all the favourable comment on Chicago's waterfront as a recreation area, this city, like Toronto, has compromised it with freeways and multi-level arterial roads. The pedestrian reaches the water via a tunnel along the canal bank. The fact that this tunnel has interesting murals can't hide the fact that access is claustrophobic and that downtown streets and waterfront are not an organic whole for the pedestrian tourist.
