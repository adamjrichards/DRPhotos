D226-223A
V11-10-10 - On the trail FX Here's my first example of what I call the photo-sketch technique. It's done in the studio after the foot slogging is over with. Not always easy to predict what might make a good p-s picture. I try to document the trip, with "standard" picture treatment, first of all. That means taking a lot of contextual pictures, not only stunning views. But the best photos frequently make the best photo sketches.

The "scenic" approach to scenery? Back in the studio I usually test a picture by printing it 11x17 and standing it up scenically with others on the mantelpiece. For artistic purposes the "good" pictures are the ones that are intelligible and interesting from across the room. What is this "across the room" quality? To my eye, it consists of a recognisable and iconic structure, and often a kind of thematic quality. How about: "Figures in a landscape" for instance? A picture doesn't have to be titled this way. But it helps me to imagine that a picture could have some such wider association. Having some inkling of a picture's thematic agenda could also help in the evaluation of the different components of a picture, and in the studio work of taking a purely photographic image and decluttering, strengthening it with line and tonal adjustments.

D226-224
09-06-18 - Terraced Inca site. We begin to pass stupendous Inca sites like this one. The upper buildings comprised storehouses and accommodation for workers and guard people. The scalloped edges below apparently had some significance in Inca iconography. Possibly to represent the coils of a serpent. The raised terrace would also be above rainy season flood levels.

D226-224A
V11-10-10 - Terraced Inca site FX This version illustrates a pitfall of using digital techniques to do something very close to what the sketcher or watercolourist often does. The tool here emphasises edges. Natural scenes frequently have plenty of broken rock and foliage, and the challenge is to restrain the edge effects to essential details. Otherwise the picture takes on the cluttered quality of an overworked sketch, one overpopulated with irrelevant details.
