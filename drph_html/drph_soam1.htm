D224-043 09-06-05 - Galapagos Cruise.

June 4-5 Quito, Ecuador

D224-006 09-06-05 - Quito arrival, surrounded by mountains.
This view from our hotel room, greeted us the morning after arrival in Quito, Ecuador. We're impressed by the city's location with mountains on all sides. Contrary to richer cities, the low-rent districts are often higher up the mountainsides, with a better view.

D224-008 09-06-05 - Hotel Dann Carlton, Quito.
The Dann Carlton is a medium-sized hotel. Owned by a Senora Spivak. A Columbian, we're told. Merle once knew a Spivak family in Toronto. The hotel is named after one of the tribes of Israel.

D224-007 09-06-05 - A major Ecuadorean export, roses.
Reproduction period furniture in the lobby. Plenty of English speakers on staff. Fresh roses, in large displays. Roses are a major Ecuadorean export, shipped all over the world by air. Russians prefer the long-stemmed variety.

D224-010 09-06-05 -
Not much scent. Too bad. Or would that lead to sensory overkill with so many clustered together?

June 5 Flight Quito-San Christobal Is.

D224-012 09-06-05 -
We catch our plane for the Galapagos at Quito airport.

D224-014 09-06-05 -
"TAME" airlines as opposed to "WILD" airlines? Reminds us of Hamilton, Mount Hope airport. The outdoor entry to the airplane, up a stairway humanizes aviation, but needs good weather.

D224-023 09-06-05 - Quito from the air.
Liftoff for the Galapagos. Our takeoff runway is to the right. Taller buildings and hotels on the left. The football stadium, the red and green oval, bottom left.

D224-025 09-06-05 - Out over the Pacific.

San Christobal (Chatham) Is., Galapagos

D224-028 09-06-05 -
An airport with landscaping and a cactus garden. Really more of a landing strip than an air terminal. Though the Galapagos is part of Ecuador, we proceed to yet another careful check of documents and luggage.

D224-031 09-06-05 -
They don't want illegal immigrants of either the human or the ecological kind.

D224-033 09-06-05 - Our first seal of many.
We're transported by mini-bus from airport to harbour. Going have to learn to tell the difference between seals and sea lions. Ears, says Merle.

June 5 Transfer to MV Santa Cruz

D224-035 09-06-05 - Our first Zodiac jaunt of many.
10 or 15 Passengers per boatload. The town has several thousand population but no piers for large ships. Everything has to be ferried in and out of harbour in small boats.

D224-039 09-06-05 - Yes, well ...
Do these people look apprehensive, or eager? The dark, reflective sunglasses are a good foil for the photographer. You can read anything you want into the reflection -- your own anxiety or excitement.

D224-040 09-06-05 - This will be ...
One of the guides fills us in on a few details about our accomodation. He's Italian-speaking and eventually leads a group from Italy.

D224-042 09-06-05 - Your home for the next 3 nights.
If this is the Santa Cruz, it looks like some of us may have to sleep on the roof.

D224-043 09-06-05 - Just kidding. THIS is it.
THERE'S the Santa Cruz. What a relief. Looks like a crowd has already gathered at the bar on the rear upper deck. Happy hour starts early in these latitudes.

D224-044 09-06-05 - The MV Santa Cruz.
The Santa Cruz is registered in Guayaquil, 1675 gross tonnage, length 72 metres, accomodation for 90 passengers, 2 x 1620 bhp engines, cruising speed 13 knots.

D224-047 09-06-05 - The crew welcomes us on board.
60 crew and 7 guides. Steerage class cabins are behind round ports on lower deck. Toffs located on the deck above, away from the engine.

D224-048 09-06-05 - Another Zodiac of tourists motors out.
Looking back at Puerto Moreno, San Christobal Island. The airport just over the hill on the horizon. ... Is that a deckchair I see? Doesn't seem to need rearranging though.

D224-051 09-06-05 -
14 passengers, 1 crew on the last zodiac out from shore.

D224-052 09-06-05 - Plus our luggage.
Our own bags, blue and red, are visible on the near side. The red one is Merle's. Lucky it didn't fall overboard.

D224-053 09-06-05 - The Zodiacs are hoisted on board.
The Santa Cruz carries 6, plus a glass bottom boat.

D224-061 09-06-05 - And stacked three high.
The messy-looking rope skirts are to protect the boat's air-inflated rubber hull against collisions with rocks during landings on shore.

D224-055 09-06-05 - We head out of the anchorage.
The wind is fair for France. Or, at least, for a trip down the coast to Cerro Brujo, our afternoon destination. Meantime, a bite of lunch.

D224-059 09-06-05 -
This is what a 13 knot bow-wave looks like. Quite a commotion of water. The Santa Cruz makes most of her mileage at lunch time and in the evenings. This positions us for our morning and afternoon excursions.

June 5, pm Wet landing, Cerro Brujo.

D224-068 09-06-05 - "Cormorants", ready for first landing.
We're divided into groups such as "Sea Lions" and "Cormorants". Each group, with a guide, fits into a single Zodiac. The small groups are easier for the guide to talk to and reduce the impact on trails and beaches ashore.

D224-071 09-06-05 -
Cerro Brujo is just down the coast from Puerto Moreno. We keep a sharp lookout for critters as we motor shoreward. D'you think those are birds in the right hand cave? Or merely bird-shaped rocks?

D224-084 09-06-05 -
A "wet landing". No pier, over the side and over our ankles. The reef offshore moderates the larger breakers, so the zodiac is stable. Wow, strange island in the offing.

D224-102 09-06-05 - "Kicker Rock".
Looks like a boot. But the kick would have to be backward, with the heel. A volcanic vestige, eroding in the sea. We're at the older, eastern side of the archipelago.

D224-088 09-06-05 - A trek to the beach.
From the zodiac landing area to the swimming beach. The first boatload already exploring water and shore. This is the 1st island Darwin visited. He slept on shore and marvelled at the volcanic cones inland.

D224-091 09-06-05 - Welcoming committee asleep.
Any more relaxed and it would spread out like a puddle and sink into the sand.

D224-096 09-06-05 -
The Santa Cruz has supplied us with swim fins, goggles and snorkels. The zodiac offshore stands ready to keep an eye on the swimmers.

D224-100a 09-06-05 - One for the Beach portfolio.
This is one of the world's more idyllic beaches. No high-rise resort hotels. No palm trees. No little thatched cabanas. No cliches.

D224-104 09-06-05 -
A few figures wander down at the far, southern, end of the beach. We've been warned by the naturalist not to go farther. They don't want to have to send out search parties or have us trampling endangered species without supervision.

D224-107 09-06-05 - Looking back toward the landing.
Looking back toward the swimming area. A seal or sea-lion is asleep on the rocks, left.

D224-109a 09-06-05 -
"Kicker Rock" again.

D224-110 09-06-05 - Cerro Brujo.
Internet translation of "Cerro Brujo" is "Wizard Hill". It has a dark, portentous quality that certainly dominates the beach area. Other parts of San Christobal Is. are flatter, less dramatic.

D224-111 09-06-05 - The Santa Cruz.
The Santa Cruz waits offshore for our return. The cooks are preparing supper, no doubt. A low afternoon sun. It gets dark about 6:15pm at this latitude.

D224-112 09-06-05 - Derek savours the beachcombing life.
Beach camping might be attractive. But not sure how you'd survive here, if you really were marooned. No fresh water that we could see. No edible vegetation or fruit. You'd have to munch critters, if you could catch them.

D224-117 09-06-05 - Merle tries snorkeling.
Gear supplied by the Santa Cruz from lockers on the upper deck. Every size flipper was available up to 13 for Derek.

D224-119 09-06-05 -
Merle, see any jetsam, flotsam, fish, turtles? Pieces of eight? Dubloons?

D224-121 09-06-05 -
Derek is unable to use the snorkel because his eyglasses won't fit inside. Being nearsighted underwater is even more useless than being nearsighted on land..

D224-147 09-06-05 - Shadows lengthen.
Moon-rise over this wind-blown dune, back of the beach, reminds us that the afternoon's excursion is nearly over.

D224-148 09-06-05 -
Time to gather our flippers, snorkels and damp towels and head back to the zodiac landing.

D224-150 09-06-05 - Back to the Santa Cruz.
A fast ride back. And noisy. Climbing up the gangway of the Santa Cruz, we have our feet hosed down so we don't bring beach sand on board. Partly for ecological reasons, partly to keep sediment out of the ship's drains.

June 6, am Espanola (Hood) Is.

D224-155 09-06-06 -
The zodiacs are lowered for another excursion. We're stationed off Punta Suarez, Espanola Island. The 1st island Darwin set eyes on. He didn't land. But Captain Fitzroy of the Beagle send out a boat to explore the shoreline.

D224-157 09-06-06 -
Other tourists are already on the point. The waves are bigger today. But you don't feel them much on the open water. Nearing shore the shelving bottom lifts the water more dramatically.

D224-158 09-06-06 -
Glimpses of plentiful animal life among the rocks: crabs and iguanas.

D224-160 09-06-06 - Today, a dry landing.
Here you see why a zodiac needs those rope blankets on the bow. The driver holds it onto the pier with the engine running. The bow takes a steady grinding from the rocks and concrete.

D224-163 09-06-06 - Marine iguanas match the rocks.
Why the iguanas should have evolved an almost chameleon-like match to the rocks is unclear. No serious predation here except for the more vulnerable babies.

D224-164 09-06-06 - Don't step on somebody's tail.
Do they cuddle together for warmth, companionship, or are they just oblivious to each other?

D224-168 09-06-06 - Assertive sea lion. The "Beachmaster".
One of our "Cormorants" attempted a close-up picture. They got rushed and barked at. But keep 10 ft away and they ignore you.

D224-171 09-06-06 - Blue-footed boobies.
These two Blue-footed Boobies are clearly up to something. They have ritualized movements, almost a kind of dance.

D224-172 09-06-06 - Friendly, curious.
They notice the tourists. It almost seems as if they're performing for our benefit.

D224-174 09-06-06 - A display for the tourists.
Say, nice wing feathers.

D224-176 09-06-06 - Oh-oh, mating ritual starting up.
The two at bottom go into their dance of love. The third one at top was trying to get the female's attention, but, sadly, was ignored.

June 6, am Espanola (Hood) Is.

D224-179 09-06-06 - Marine iguanas.
They seem to be basking on the sand. Get up sluggards. Whatever happened to "Nature, red in tooth and claw?" Maybe iguanas simply groove on tropic beaches, just like some snow-bird travellers I could name.

D224-180 09-06-06 -
Seems like beauty is in the eye of the beholden. I'm beholden to you, Merle, for bringing me to this place.

D224-181 09-06-06 - Not a land iguana?
Probably the Espanola Lava Lizard according to the Metropolitan Touring guidebook, p37. The travel literature provided by this company is outstanding. And written in good English too.

D224-184 09-06-06 - Marine iguanas.
I can't believe that this kind of cosy pile-up can be explained by anything other than fellow-feeling. To us they seem so ugly, we can hardly believe they might be capable of mutual toleration, or even affection.

D224-185 09-06-06 -
The white patches are probably salt-encrusted moulting skin. Each time they emerge from the ocean, water evaporates, leaving behind salt crystals. Another reason why they often seem to have a white cap is that they have a way of purging excess salt from their systems by sneezing it out of their nostrils.

D224-187 09-06-06 - Staying near the water for foraging.
They feed on weed in the shallows near shore. The rest of the time is spent resting or ... you know. But we didn't see any of that kind of behaviour. Odd. What else is there to do in paradise?

D224-189 09-06-06 - Kayo? (My friend the bird-expert.)
A Masked Booby perhaps?

D224-195 09-06-06 - Sea lion, bottom right.
We follow a nature trail along the shoreline cliff. 10 to 15ft above the water, but occasionally cooled by mist from breaking waves.

D224-198 09-06-06 - The white cliffs of Hood Is.
We know why the white cliffs of the Galapagos are white. In time, the combined droppings of birds, seals, and sea-lions adds a permanent, lighter varnish to the dark rocks.

D224-200 09-06-06 -
The birds seem little concerned with tourists pointing cameras. However, the guides are vigilant in keeping us on the path and in small spaced-out groups.

D224-201 09-06-06 - Just picking up a few rays.
This seal Looks a little spaced out too. Or perhaps, blissed out.

D224-205 09-06-06 - Frigate bird.
A Frigate bird adds a piratical touch to our "peaceful Kingdom" scene. Those "W" profile wings and the hooked beak bode no good for eggs and small fry.

D224-207 09-06-06 - Kayo! Masked Booby?
Is there such a thing as a Grey-footed Booby?

D224-208 09-06-06 - Albatross, incubating.
The path leads to a major Albatross nesing area. Birds lay a single egg on the ground and incubate it on the spot. No nest-building.

D224-209 09-06-06 - Why does the albatross cross the road?
We're cautioned by our guide to wait till the traffic signal turns green. Meanwhile the albatross has right of way. It proceeds with quiet dignity to cross the hiking path.

D224-210 09-06-06 - To get to its mate, silly.
Such a sound of bills clacking. I'm told each pair has a joyous reunion here after months at sea.

D224-211 09-06-06 - This is not an albatross duel...
Espanola Island is said to be home base to 30,000 birds.

D224-212 09-06-06 - but a courtship dance.
The birds are taking-off and whistling in for a landing so frequently the guide calls it: Albatross International Airport. We hikers have to stay off the flight path.

D224-213 09-06-06 - These birds mate for life.

D224-215 09-06-06 - The stick marks the hiker path.
Patiently sitting on an egg.
