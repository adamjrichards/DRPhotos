Tegernsee 2, Bauer in der Au
Bauer in der Au is the title, in the HF literature, of today's ramble. The name appears to mean Farmer in the dell. After considerable web searching, found that the term brings up an actual Tegernsee tourist guesthouse, the proprietor's zoning troubles with the municipality, and also information that the term au is Bavarian or Austrian dialect for meadow. Au is not listed in some major, on-line, German-to-English dictionaries.

Anyone recall the nursery rhyme?

The farmer in the dell, (2x)
Hi-ho the dairy-oh,
The farmer in the dell.

The farmer takes a wife, (2x)
Hi-ho ... (etc.)

The wife takes the cat, ...

... cat ... mouse ... cheese ... (each verse, someone is pointed out in succession.)

The cheese stands alone ...

I think this last is the "gotcha" line. The person selected is either "out" or voted into public office. We voted ourselves into carrying on with the ramble, despite misty, drizzly weather. Walking is quite feasible under these conditions, provided you have suitable raingear and stick to lower altitudes.

D366-095 15-09-08
Bauern omlette for DR.
A real man-of-the-people, authentic, salt-of-the-earth, farmery kind of dude. Oh yes, and a radler to wash it down.

The name "radler" means "cyclist" and was invented by a Bavarian innkeeper whose establishment was overwhelmed one day by cyclists. Having limited beer, and an overstock of lemon soda in the basement, he mixed beer and soda 50-50, with the claim that they wouldn't fall off their bikes on the rest of the ride. The new drink immediately caught on.

Unfortunately, ready-mix radler is hard-to-find in North America. And waiters, typically, don't know what you mean by shandy. (That's my glass, discreetly placed at the right of the pic.)
