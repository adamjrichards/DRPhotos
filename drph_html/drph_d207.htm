D207-032 08-05-12 - RMC seen from Fort Henry.
2008 May 12, Monday We catch the bus for our Ottawa trip at 7:30am. Many senior citizens aboard, some with walkers. One lady, proudly 91. Welcome to the rest of your life, Derek. Weather outlook: cloudy to cloudy bright.
Kingston, Fort Henry. Wonderful view, commanding situation. Once again, viewing the massive grey those limestone walls: "killer professionalism". Compare this strongpoint to Fort Erie, which, to me, has a sort of country churchyard feel. And yet, quaint Fort Erie saw ferocious battles and major loss of life, while Fort Henry never fired a shot in anger.

Lunch in the Fort Henry officers' mess, built into the outer wall of the fort. Back on the bus, finish reading Silas Marner by George Eliot. A feel-good ending. Not quite what the squire's son wanted, but Epie stays with her childhood sweetheart, Silas, and they are married. I've enjoyed the book, oddly perhaps, for its slightly archaic language and sensibility.
What is it that makes this pre-industrial life seem so ...? Attractive isn't quite the word. We don't actually want to live that way, but we sense something about it that we wish we could find in our own lives. I recall also the "wise rustics" in Far from the Madding crowd. Idyllic rural characters, and wise.

D207-120 08-05-13 - The War Museum.
2008 May 13, Tuesday To the War Museum next. Sometime after the Boer War and WW1, I Choke up completely. Nothing but death and destruction and despair. Depression in wide-angled 3d exhibits. I escape to the "fin" of the museum, a soaring gallery with windows placed so you can view the Parliament buildings from a special vantage point. At a certain time of year, a sunbeam (ray of hope) enters through an opening in the roof and falls on a certain part of the memorial artwork.

Time to flee downstairs to the tank museum. where the hardware of destruction seems less panoramic, more impersonal. But the pall of despair is hard to throw off. Death in war always seems more real to me than cowboy shoot-em-ups, murder mystery mayhem, or crime in the streets. This is probably what comes of being a war-time child with a daddy in the forces.

D207-198 08-05-14 - West Coast native culture rules.
2008 May 14, Wednesday The Museum of Civilization today. The Great Hall, always a stunning experience. Is it fair to other aboriginal communities that the Haida, the Tsimshian, the Kwakiutl get top national billing? Certainly on ground of raw power over the specator, the emphasis seems justified. This really is a cathedral. I could almost feel this was part of my heritage. Oh what a treasure we've destroyed in cutting down all those West Coast trees for plywood when they could have been used for totems and monumental lodges. Can we only live an authentic life today by imagining a vanished world like this. In my own head I can sometimes hear the old county accents of Hardy's "wise rustics". But is that enough and how do I combine those accents with Haida totems, Greek temples and Gothic cathedrals?

What is my heritage? Rootless cosmopolitanism? Wild colonial boy? In the middle of this room, I feel nostalgia for an "old country", a home town, a native land, that I can hardly define.

This monumental west coast culture has declined and transformed into something else, but we have at least witnessed the process, while not being able to stop or reverse it. There exists a relatively rich store of normally ephemeral artifacts, stories, eye-witness accounts, photographs, even. If these leavings that we see here are so evocative, consider what has been lost from the ancient cultures of the Middle East, the Mediterranean, Asia, where usually only the most durable and monumental remains give witness to the past.

A satisfying lunch on the museum patio overlooking the Ottawa River and the back of the Parliament Buildings. Under blossoming crabs with pink petals falling in our soup. Joggers going by on the path below. Wow, was that a streaker. No, not quite, but we're certainly seeing more of the ladies in the sunshine today.

Hours of driving in the bus to our last pit stop, the old 1837 resto on highway 401. Now a Tim Hortons. I sit on a concrete curb in the parking lot, tractor trailers roaring in back, the highway roaring in front. Every holiday seems to end with a ghastly dose of this reality. Highways, parking lots, fast food, idling engines seems to symbolize everything that's wrong with civilization. Museum of Civilization, why not an exhibit of this?
