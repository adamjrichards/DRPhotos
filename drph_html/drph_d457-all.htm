D457-016 17-09-26 - With a branch line, like RR points. These are too clearly artificial to be a natural rock formation. Speculation was that they were created by sledges or wheeled carts hauling stone from a nearby quarry. Even today there is a quarry in the immediate area.

websrch:cart ruts malta


The page below has a good essay on the interpretation of the cart ruts.

websrch:coppens cart ruts


As a rank novice in the field, I favour what I call the "track weathering" theory. At one time today's barren rock surface probably was covered with more soil. This was gradually eroded away due to the neolithic hunger for firewood and the later need for charcoal to support bronze and iron-age smelting. Very early cart tracks would speedily wear down to underlying bedrock and over the centuries trap water which would very slowly dissolve the underlying stone, like potholes in today's roads.

The above article also noted that the local limestone can be water-absorbent and more subject to crushing and wear when wet. The groves, one initiated, would tend to grow, even today without significant traffic, by trapping rain water, but unlike the surrounding randomly eroded surface, now in a definite line. The present day grooves, therefore, might be thought of as more of an image of early cart or sledge routes, than excavated tracks.
